The Hidden Language of Ancient Forests

Beneath the leaf litter and tangled roots lies an invisible network — a wood-wide web of fungal threads that trees use to whisper, trade, and care for one another.

Elara Woods

Forest Ecologist & Writer · View profile

May 12, 2025 12 min read

Morning light filters through a centuries-old beech forest in the Białowieża Primeval Forest, Poland. Photo: Unsplash

There is a conversation happening beneath your feet. Right now, as you walk through a park or a forest, billions of fungal threads are carrying signals between trees — sugar and water and distress calls — through a network so intricate and efficient that scientists have begun calling it the internet of the forest. The trees, it turns out, are not the solitary competitors we imagined. They are, in many ways, profoundly social.

This discovery — still relatively young in the long annals of forest ecology — has upended centuries of thinking about how plants live and compete. The old model was Darwinian in a blunt sense: each tree for itself, fighting for light and water, the strongest canopy winning. What we are learning instead is something far stranger and more beautiful.

The Mycelial Web

At the heart of this story is the mycorrhizal fungus — a word derived from the Greek mykes (fungus) and rhiza (root). These fungi form extraordinary partnerships with the roots of trees, extending far beyond what any single root system could reach. In exchange for sugars that trees produce through photosynthesis, the fungi provide trees with water, phosphorus, nitrogen, and other minerals they'd otherwise struggle to absorb.

But the partnership goes further than simple trade. The fungal threads — called hyphae — connect the roots of different trees across a forest floor, forming a continuous underground web. Through this web, trees can share resources. A large, sun-catching tree will pump sugars down through its roots and into the network, where they travel to a seedling struggling in the shade below.

"The forest is not a collection of individual trees — it is a community, with elders and young ones, strong and weak, and a network of relationships that we are only beginning to understand." — Suzanne Simard, Forest Ecologist, UBC

The researcher most associated with this discovery is Suzanne Simard, a Canadian forest ecologist whose 1997 paper in Nature was among the first to demonstrate that trees exchange carbon through mycorrhizal networks. Her work showed that Douglas firs and paper birches — species that were considered competitors — were actually in constant dialogue, their fates intertwined through the soil.

The Mother Tree

Simard coined the term "mother tree" to describe the largest, oldest trees in a forest — those that are most deeply embedded in the mycorrhizal network and most active in sharing resources. These trees, she found, send extra carbon to their own kin: seedlings that are genetically related to them receive more support than strangers.¹

When a mother tree is dying — stressed by disease, drought, or logging — she appears to increase the flow of carbon and defence signals to her seedlings and neighbours. It is as if, knowing her end is near, she pours what remains of herself into the network. The forest remembers her long after she falls.

This challenges us to think differently about tree felling. When we remove the oldest trees first — as many commercial forestry operations do, targeting the largest timber — we are not simply removing one tree. We are removing the hub of the network. The connections weaken. The young trees, deprived of the flow of resources and signals, grow more slowly and are more vulnerable to stress.

Distress Signals and Chemical Warnings

The wood-wide web carries more than sugar. Trees under attack from insects or pathogens release chemical signals — both through the air and through the root network — that alert their neighbours. The receiving trees begin producing defensive compounds before any pest has even arrived.

Willows attacked by caterpillars, for instance, increase the tannin content in their leaves — making them harder to digest. Nearby willows not yet under attack do the same, apparently in response to airborne volatile compounds released by their stressed neighbours. Similar responses have been documented in dozens of species.

The forest is not a collection of individual trees. It is a community with elders and young ones — and a communication system as old as the land itself.

What is still debated among scientists is whether this constitutes something like intention. Are trees choosing to share? Are they aware, in any meaningful sense, of the network they inhabit? Most ecologists are careful here, resisting the pull of anthropomorphism. But the behaviours are real, documented, and reproducible. The forest acts, in many ways, as a single organism.

What This Means for Us

These findings have begun to filter into forestry practice and conservation policy, though slowly. Some forest managers now argue for leaving old trees standing as "legacy trees" — anchors for the mycorrhizal network that allow it to persist and regenerate after disturbance. Others advocate for preserving the full range of tree species in a forest, since different species host different fungal networks, and diversity strengthens the system as a whole.

For those of us who are not foresters, this knowledge offers something different: a new way of being in a forest. To walk among old trees knowing they are in conversation changes the experience. The silence is not absence. It is a language you cannot hear.

Stand still long enough, and you might feel it anyway — that sense of something deep and unhurried moving beneath you. That is the forest, going about its ancient business. Feeding its young. Caring for its sick. Remembering its dead.

References

¹ Simard, S.W. et al. (2015). "Resource transfer between plants through ectomycorrhizal fungal networks." Forest Ecology and Management, 352, 14–20.
